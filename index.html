How to read him

    Methodology & sources

    How the numbers are built. Each culture gets a base score per metric, which I set by hand using the direction of established cross-cultural research (below). Your filter picks (religion / education / politics) then nudge that base up or down. The result is a modeled estimate — reproducible from the logic, but not measured from real daters. No individual data was collected.

    Frameworks that inform the direction

    • Hofstede cultural dimensions — Individualism, Masculinity, Long-Term Orientation, Power Distance. Informs family involvement, bill-paying, commitment. hofstede-insights.com
    • Erin Meyer, The Culture Map — high- vs low-context communication. Informs the "says what he means" directness score. erinmeyer.com
    • Pew Research — Global Attitudes / religiosity — how central religion is by country. Informs the religion filter and commitment. pewresearch.org
    • World Values Survey — gender-role attitudes, family importance. Informs bill-paying and family. worldvaluessurvey.org
    • Gary Chapman, The 5 Love Languages — the five categories only. The by-nationality split has no supporting dataset.

    Where it is NOT valid — be honest

    • The exact percentage is fake-precise. "85%" means "high," not a measured rate. Read scores as bands (low / mixed / high), not decimals.
    • The love-language distribution has no dataset. Illustrative only.
    • The filter adjustments (religion / education / politics) are directional guesses, not fitted to data.
    • No dataset exists at this granularity (culture × religion × education × politics). Those cells are interpolated.
    • Cultures are broad averages. Any individual can be the complete opposite. This is a conversation starter, never a verdict on a real person.
    These are illustrative cultural patterns for fun and orientation — not scientific data, and never a substitute for knowing the actual person. Every individual is different. Use it as a conversation starter, not a verdict. Made in Bali.
